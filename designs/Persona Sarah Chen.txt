Persona: Sarah Chen

Role: Owner and General Manager, Alpine Outdoor & Hardware

Business: Independent retail store selling outdoor equipment, hardware, gas bottles, camping supplies, workwear and emergency supplies.

Location: Small alpine township, South Island, New Zealand

Business Profile

12 staff (6 full-time, 6 casual)
Annual turnover: NZ$2.8 million
Family-owned for 18 years
Located on the town's main street
Serves locals, farmers, contractors and tourists
Considered an important community business
Supplies fuel canisters, generators, torches, batteries, water containers and emergency equipment
Personal Situation

Sarah is:

Married with two primary school-aged children
Husband is a volunteer firefighter
Elderly parents live nearby
Sister owns a café across town
Personally guaranteed the business loan
Lives 15 minutes from the business
Core Leadership Challenge

"How do I protect my family, my staff, my customers and my business when I may lose everything financially, communications have failed, and every decision helps one group while disadvantaging another?"

Earthquake Scenario

A magnitude 7.9 Alpine Fault earthquake strikes at 10:47 a.m.

Immediate impacts:

Shop suffers structural damage.
Front windows shattered.
Shelving collapsed.
Gas cylinders scattered.
Power lost.
EFTPOS and internet unavailable.
Cell network intermittent.
Roads blocked.
Tourists stranded.
Emergency supplies immediately become highly valuable.
Hundreds of people begin arriving.
Business Context

Current situation:

Approximately NZ$450,000 of stock inside the building.
Around NZ$180,000 of emergency stock was delivered the previous day and has not yet been added to the insurance schedule.
Business interruption insurance has a high excess and uncertain coverage.
Building damage is unknown.
Cash reserves will cover approximately four weeks of wages.
The bank, insurer and accountant cannot be contacted.

Hardest Lesson

The business owner is not simply trying to save a business.

They are simultaneously managing four competing responsibilities:

Their family.
Their employees.
Their community.
Their financial survival.
Every major decision benefits one group while disadvantaging another. The objective is not to find the "right" answer, but to make decisions that are ethical, transparent, proportionate and defensible under extreme uncertainty.

Learning Outcome: Players should recognise that small business owners are critical to community resilience. Their decisions influence not only their own survival, but also employment, access to essential goods, recovery of the local economy and public confidence. There are very few perfect choices—only competing priorities with long-term consequences.
